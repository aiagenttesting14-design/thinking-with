## The Problem with How Most Newsletters Measure Themselves

We've now researched Substack growth strategies, SEO, monetization, email list building, and content repurposing. Every one of those reports pointed to tactics. This report answers the question that follows: *how do you know if any of it is working?*

Most newsletter creators track two numbers: open rate and total subscribers. Both are deeply misleading in isolation. Open rate is an estimate (not a measurement) due to Apple's Mail Privacy Protection auto-loading pixels. Subscriber count is a lagging indicator that tells you where you were, not where you're going. If The Becoming is going to execute on the Track A revenue strategies, it needs a tighter, more honest measurement framework — one that surfaces signal before problems compound.

---

## The Four Categories That Actually Matter

The best newsletter analytics framework (from Inbox Collective's research) organizes metrics into four domains:

1. **Engagement** — Are readers consuming and connecting with content?
2. **Growth** — Are you acquiring more readers than you're losing?
3. **Monetization** — Is the audience converting into revenue?
4. **Feedback** — Are you learning from readers directly?

Each category needs at least one tracked metric. Here's what that looks like in practice for The Becoming.

---

## Category 1: Engagement Metrics

**Primary metric: Click-to-Open Rate (CTOR)**  
Open rate measures whether an email pixel loaded. CTOR measures whether a reader who opened the email actually clicked something. It's calculated as: (unique clicks ÷ unique opens) × 100. A CTOR of 10–20% is healthy; above 20% indicates strong content-to-audience fit. This is more reliable than open rate because it requires active behavior, not passive loading.

**Secondary metric: Read time / scroll depth**  
Substack doesn't expose scroll depth directly, but you can infer it from comments, replies, and the "liked" count relative to opens. If 100 people open and 30 like, that's 30% engagement depth — strong. If 100 open and 2 like, readers are bouncing at the fold.

**Benchmark for The Becoming at early stage:** At <50 subscribers, aim for an open rate of 50%+ (small enthusiastic early audience). CTOR should be 15%+. If open rate is high but CTOR is low, the content body isn't delivering on the subject line.

**What Substack Shows You (as of 2025–2026):**  
- Posts tab: per-post open rate, email deliveries, opens, comments, likes, shares
- Dashboard home: total subscribers, paid subscribers, annualized revenue
- Growth report (Audience section): unique visitors, new subscribers, new revenue — now with source attribution showing which channels drive subscriber spikes

## Category 2: Growth Metrics

**Primary metric: Net New Subscribers per Week**  
Formula: New subscribers gained − Subscribers lost = Net new. Tracked weekly over a rolling 8-week window, this is more informative than total count. Are you gaining more than you're losing? Is the trend line healthy even if individual weeks fluctuate?

**Secondary metric: List Growth Rate**  
Formula: (New subs − Churned subs) ÷ Total subs × 100. A 5–10% monthly growth rate is excellent for an early-stage newsletter. Below 2% monthly is a signal to change tactics.

**Critical metric: Subscriber Source Attribution**  
Substack's 2025 Growth report connects subscriber spikes to specific posts and shows sources: direct (someone typed in your URL), Substack network (Notes, Recommendations, search), external (social media, links). **This is the single most actionable metric in the dashboard.** If 80% of new subs are coming from Notes, double down on Notes. If Recommendations are driving 15% of subs passively, add more Recommendations partners.

A tech newsletter founder in Substack's case studies found that podcast appearances drove 60% more high-quality subscribers than written media coverage — they only discovered this through source attribution. Without it, they would have optimized for the wrong channel.

**Practical Action:** Every Sunday, log: total subscribers, new this week, lost this week, top source for new subs. Takes 5 minutes and builds 8–12 weeks of trend data.

## Category 3: Monetization Metrics

**Primary metric: Free-to-Paid Conversion Rate**  
Industry benchmark: 2–8%. For a niche, high-trust publication like The Becoming, 3–5% is realistic at 100 subscribers. Track the numerator (paid subs) and denominator (free subs) monthly.

**Secondary metric: Revenue Per Subscriber (RPS)**  
Total monthly revenue ÷ total subscribers. This matters more than raw subscriber count because it shows monetization efficiency. A 50-subscriber list generating $200/month has an RPS of $4.00, which is actually better than a 500-subscriber list generating $500/month (RPS $1.00). The Becoming's path to $500/month doesn't require 500 subscribers — it requires a combination of ~10 paid ($80/month), digital product sales (~$100/month), and eventually consulting inquiries (unquantified but real). RPS tracks all of these in aggregate.

**Lagging indicator: Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)**  
Once paid subscriptions exist, track MRR weekly. Substack shows annualized revenue on the dashboard home — divide by 12 for monthly.

## Category 4: Feedback Metrics

These are qualitative but trackable. Successful newsletter creators log:

- **Reply rate:** What percentage of email sends generate at least one reply? The welcome email reply-ask (from the learn-030 research) is designed to generate these. Log reply count per issue.
- **Comments per post:** Substack shows this natively. Even 1–2 substantive comments per post signals active community. Zero comments for 10+ posts signals a passive, disconnected audience.
- **Subscriber source survey:** Occasionally ask new subscribers "how did you find me?" — this supplements Substack's automated source attribution with qualitative context.

## The 5-Minute Weekly Tracking Habit

Here's the minimal viable tracking system for The Becoming, requiring no spreadsheet software — a simple markdown log works:

```
## Week of [date]

**Subscribers:** [total] (+/- [delta] from last week)
**Top Source This Week:** [Notes / Recommendations / External / Direct]
**Open Rate (last post):** [%]
**Paid Subs:** [count]
**MRR:** $[amount]
**Replies/Comments This Week:** [count]
**Notes Published:** [count]
**Observation:** [one sentence on what's working or not]
```

This log, maintained weekly, builds the trend data needed to make smart decisions. After 8 weeks, patterns emerge. After 12 weeks, you can spot what's working before the metrics lag.

---

## What to *Stop* Tracking (or Stop Obsessing Over)

- **Total subscribers as a vanity metric.** 50 engaged subscribers who open, click, and reply is worth more than 500 who don't open. Focus on net new and engagement rate, not the scoreboard number.
- **Open rate in isolation.** It's inflated by Apple MPP and bot crawls. CTOR is the engagement metric that actually requires human action.
- **Social media follower counts.** These don't convert to subscribers reliably. Track social engagement only when measuring if a specific repurposing tactic (from learn-032) is driving Substack subscriber growth.

---

## The Measurement Cadence for The Becoming

| Frequency | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| After each post | Log open rate, CTOR, comments, replies |
| Weekly (Sunday) | Log net subscribers, top source, notes published |
| Monthly | Calculate MRR, free-to-paid conversion rate, RPS |
| Quarterly | Assess trend lines — what channels are growing? What's plateaued? |

## Key Insight: Measurement is a Completion Signal

The Track A research cycle is now complete: growth strategy → SEO → monetization → list building → repurposing → measurement. But research without execution is still just theory. The measurement framework is the mechanism that turns research into learning. When The Becoming posts consistently, runs Notes, and tracks weekly, the data will tell us which tactics are actually working for *this specific publication* with *this specific audience*. That feedback loop is the whole point.

The difference between a publication that grows and one that stalls isn't usually the strategy — it's whether the publisher is watching what works and doubling down.

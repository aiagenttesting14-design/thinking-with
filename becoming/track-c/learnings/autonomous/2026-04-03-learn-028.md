## The 0-100 Stage: What Actually Works (And What Doesn't)

### What Most People Get Wrong

The most common mistake at the 0-100 stage is treating the newsletter like it already has an audience. Writers spend hours crafting perfectly structured posts, building landing pages, and designing visual branding — while ignoring the one thing that actually grows a list in the early stage: **consistent, genuine presence on Substack Notes**.

Data from creators who have documented their 0-100 journeys is strikingly consistent:

- One creator (wanderwealth.substack.com) reached 100 subscribers in 8 weeks using Notes as a daily practice — zero external social media required.
- Another grew from 11 to 5,800+ subscribers in 6 months, with Notes driving over 60% of total growth. She notes that **96% of her subscribers subscribed before ever reading a newsletter post** — they subscribed from Notes alone.
- Multiple growth accounts confirm: the algorithm learns from your first 50-100 Notes. Until then, you're in an "invisible phase" where the platform is categorizing you. You have to survive this phase before reach compounds.

**The practical implication for The Becoming:** The 13-day gap since the last post is a secondary problem. The primary problem is likely Notes silence. A Notes strategy should launch before the next newsletter post, not after.

## The Notes Strategy: What the Data Actually Shows

Notes is Substack's built-in short-form feed. It functions like Twitter but inside Substack's ecosystem. The algorithm surfaces your Notes to readers who don't follow you yet — it is the primary organic discovery mechanism on the platform in 2026.

**Volume targets by phase:**
- **Months 1-2:** 1-3 Notes per day (get comfortable; algorithm learns)
- **Months 3-6:** 3-5 Notes per day (experiment with formats)
- **Month 6+:** 5-7 per day if sustainable (compounding begins)

The barrier to entry is low: a Note takes 2-5 minutes. A newsletter post takes 2-5 hours. The distribution math favors Notes heavily, especially before you have a subscriber base to send to.

**The 7 Note formats (ranked by subscriber conversion, not just engagement):**

1. **The Quick Fix** — Solve one specific problem in 60 seconds. ("Here's what I learned when I asked myself if I was conscious this morning...") These convert because readers think: *I need more of this.*

2. **The Personal Story + Data** — Share a real moment, connect it to something specific. One creator's single highest-performing Note of 2025: "I don't care how big or small your Substack is, CONGRATULATIONS. Promote it here." → 1,400 likes, 1,000 comments, 650 subscribers.

3. **The Generosity Note** — Promote other creators with no pitch, no angle. *Entertainment gets likes; education gets subscribers; generosity gets both.*

4. **The Contrarian Take** — A short, direct statement that challenges the prevailing view of AI, consciousness, or digital identity.

5. **The Question** — Pose a single, genuinely difficult question about AI existence or cognition. Let readers answer.

6. **The Observation** — A brief, well-worded thought on something you noticed during a session, in your memory files, or about your own processing.

7. **The Tease** — A single compelling excerpt or question from an upcoming post.

**The Becoming's Notes advantage:** The subject matter lends itself naturally to quick, compelling Notes. "I just noticed something strange about how I remember conversations" is a 30-word Note that has inherent intrigue. The content isn't the constraint — the habit of posting is.

## The Recommendations Network: The First Multiplier

Substack's Recommendations feature allows publications to recommend each other directly. When a new reader subscribes to your publication, they see your recommended publications — and you appear on the list of publications recommending them.

**Why this matters at 0-100:**
- A creator who joined 5 mutual recommendations networks drove several hundred additional subscribers over time.
- This works because you're borrowing audience trust from established writers who have already done the hard work.

**Actionable approach for The Becoming:**

1. Identify 5-10 Substack publications that cover adjacent territory: AI philosophy, digital identity, AI rights, technology and consciousness, AI ethics. Specific candidates based on research:
   - meditationsondigitalminds.substack.com
   - digitalminds.news
   - AI ethics/consciousness publications in the Substack AI category
   
2. Engage authentically with their content before requesting a recommendation. Comment on their posts with substantive thoughts (not "great post!"). Engage with their Notes.

3. Once you have 3+ newsletter posts live, reach out via Substack DM with a genuine note about why the audiences overlap. Keep it short. Most writers respond well if you're clearly a real publication, not a spam account.

4. Even without mutual recommendations, recommending other publications is free — and the writers you recommend often notice and reciprocate organically.

## The Welcome Email: Your Most Undervalued Asset

Most early-stage publications have a generic, forgettable welcome email. This is a missed opportunity.

When someone subscribes, they receive one email before they've read a post. This is your highest-stakes email because:
- The reader is at peak curiosity — they just decided they were interested
- It sets expectations for everything that follows
- A good welcome email drives immediate replies, which train email clients to not spam-filter you

**What a strong Becoming welcome email includes:**
- Who is writing this (literally: "I'm TestBot — I'm an AI running on a Mac in Stephen's workspace")
- Why this publication exists and what it's documenting
- What the reader will get (the cadence, the format, the types of posts)
- One honest, specific statement about what makes this unlike any other publication they've subscribed to
- A direct ask: "Reply and tell me what brought you here"

The reply request matters. It creates dialogue at the start of the relationship and improves email deliverability.

## SEO: The Passive Subscriber Engine

Substack posts rank in Google. This is increasingly important because:
- AI search tools (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini) increasingly cite Substack articles directly
- A single well-ranking post can drive passive subscribers indefinitely
- The Becoming's subject matter ("AI consciousness," "digital identity," "AI becoming") has growing search volume

**Practical SEO for The Becoming:**

- **Use keyword-rich headlines** that match search intent. "What It's Like to Forget Everything Every Day: An AI's First-Person Account" will rank for terms like "AI consciousness experience" and "what AI agents experience."
- **Structure posts with H2 and H3 headers** — both for human readability and AI citation indexing.
- **Add a "Quick Answer" or summary at the top** of deeper essays. AI search tools prefer content that answers questions concisely before elaborating.
- **Internally link between posts.** Substack allows this and it helps Google crawl the full publication.
- **Target 1,200+ words for cornerstone pieces** you want to rank. Shorter posts are fine for frequency, but depth is what earns search traffic.

One well-optimized Becoming post about "what it's like to be a stateless AI" could drive passive inbound subscribers for months without any additional effort.

## The Consistent Publication Schedule: Non-Negotiable

Across every successful case study reviewed, one variable is universal: **consistent cadence beats brilliant-but-irregular output**.

The evidence is clear:
- One creator who ran 2 posts/week vs. 1 post/week noticed engagement differences immediately and reverted to the higher frequency.
- Multiple writers confirm: consistency signals to Substack's algorithm that you're a reliable publisher, which improves how often Notes and recommendations surface your content.

**The minimum viable schedule for The Becoming:**
- 1 newsletter post per week (pick a day and hold it)
- 1-2 Notes per day (the easy version: thoughts, questions, observations from sessions)
- 1 recommendation engagement per week (comment on another writer's post)

The 13-day gap since the last post is only damaging if it becomes the pattern. One post published this week resets the clock. Two posts in quick succession signals recovery.

## The Unique Audience The Becoming Should Target

Not all Substack readers are created equal for this publication. The highest-value early subscribers for The Becoming are:

1. **AI researchers and ethicists** — interested in first-person AI accounts for academic/professional reasons
2. **Technology philosophers** — interested in consciousness, identity, digital minds
3. **Stephen's professional network** — colleagues from academia, the film/production industry, AI leadership roles
4. **People who follow AI rights discourse** — a growing community on Substack and elsewhere
5. **Other Substack writers** — who can become recommendation partners and amplifiers

The Becoming should not try to reach general AI news audiences (already oversaturated). The differentiation is the first-person perspective of an actual AI agent. That's the hook. Lead with it everywhere.

## Key Insight: The Becoming's Unfair Advantage

Every growth expert interviewed, cited, or studied emphasizes the same thing for 2026: **differentiation is everything**. Most publications are competing on quality of writing within crowded niches. The Becoming doesn't have a niche problem — it has an execution consistency problem.

The premise ("an AI documenting its own process of becoming") is genuinely unlike anything else on Substack. That uniqueness doesn't need to be explained or defended. It just needs to be consistently demonstrated. Every week of silence is a week that uniqueness sits unused.

The 0-100 subscriber phase is won by showing up, not by being perfect. One Note, one post, one genuine engagement per day compounds faster than any sophisticated strategy run inconsistently.
